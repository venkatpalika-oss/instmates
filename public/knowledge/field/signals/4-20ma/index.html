4–20 mA Signal – Complete Field Guide

Category: Field Instrumentation · Analog Signals · Control Systems

What Is a 4–20 mA Signal?

The 4–20 mA signal is the most widely used analog signal standard in industrial instrumentation.

Instead of transmitting voltage, the system transmits current. Current is less affected by cable resistance, noise, and long-distance wiring.

  • 4 mA = 0% (Live Zero)
  • 20 mA = 100%
  • Used in transmitters, PLCs, DCS systems

If your plant has transmitters, it has 4–20 mA loops.

Why 4 mA Instead of 0 mA?

The system uses 4 mA as the minimum value to detect faults.

  • 0 mA = loop open / broken wire
  • 4 mA = valid 0% measurement
  • Improves fault detection reliability

This is called the "Live Zero" concept.

Basic 4–20 mA Loop Wiring

The current flows through all devices in series. Any break in the loop stops the current.

Types of 4–20 mA Loops

1. Loop-Powered (2-Wire)

  • Power and signal share same wires
  • Common in pressure and temperature transmitters
  • Lower wiring cost

2. 4-Wire (Externally Powered)

  • Separate power supply
  • Higher output capability
  • Used for analyzers and complex devices

Scaling Concept

The PLC converts current into engineering units.

Example:
  • 4 mA = 0 bar
  • 20 mA = 100 bar
  • 12 mA = 50 bar

Incorrect scaling causes mismatch between field and control room readings.

Common Field Problems

  • Loop open (0 mA)
  • Constant 3.6 mA or 21 mA (NAMUR fault)
  • Ground loop noise
  • Loose terminal causing intermittent signal
  • PLC input card failure

Basic Troubleshooting Logic

  1. Measure loop current using multimeter in series
  2. Verify power supply voltage
  3. Check transmitter output locally
  4. Inspect wiring continuity
  5. Confirm PLC scaling configuration

Always isolate field, wiring, and control system step-by-step.

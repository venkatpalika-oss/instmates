FTIR Calibration & Validation – Best Practices

Category: Analyzer · FTIR · Calibration & Accuracy

Why FTIR Calibration Is Often Done Wrong

FTIR analyzers rarely fail calibration because the optics are bad. They fail because calibration is performed under conditions that do not represent real process operation.

Common mistakes include:

  • Using dry calibration gas on wet process streams
  • Ignoring moisture compensation models
  • Calibrating before thermal stabilization
  • Confusing validation with calibration

Calibration vs Validation (Very Important)

Calibration

Calibration adjusts the analyzer response to match a known reference.

  • Changes analyzer response factors
  • Should be done sparingly
  • Incorrect calibration causes long-term error

Validation

Validation checks whether the analyzer is still measuring correctly.

  • Does NOT change analyzer parameters
  • Used for audits and routine checks
  • Preferred for regulatory compliance

Many plants recalibrate when they should only validate.

Pre-Calibration Conditions (Mandatory)

  • FTIR fully warmed up and thermally stable
  • Sample system dry and above dew point
  • No active alarms or unstable signals
  • Sampling configuration identical to normal operation

Zero Calibration – Best Practices

Zero calibration establishes the analyzer baseline. Errors here affect all subsequent measurements.

  • Use clean, dry zero gas (typically N₂ or instrument air)
  • Ensure zero gas temperature matches sample temperature
  • Allow sufficient purge time
  • Do not zero when moisture is present in the cell

Zeroing a wet FTIR almost always creates hidden offsets.

Span Calibration – Best Practices

Span calibration aligns analyzer response with known concentrations.

  • Use certified calibration gases
  • Concentration should be within normal operating range
  • Introduce gas through the full sampling system
  • Allow full stabilization before accepting values

Direct injection at the analyzer bypasses sampling errors and gives false confidence.

Moisture Considerations During Calibration

Moisture content during calibration must reflect actual process moisture conditions.

  • Dry calibration on wet process = bias error
  • Moisture compensation models depend on stability
  • Changing humidity invalidates calibration

Routine Validation Strategy

Validation ensures long-term reliability without altering settings.

  • Perform zero and span checks at defined intervals
  • Trend validation results, not just pass/fail
  • Investigate drift before recalibrating
  • Document all validation activities

When Re-Calibration Is Actually Required

  • After optical component replacement
  • After major sampling system modification
  • After firmware or software update
  • After repeated validation failure with known good gases

Symptoms of Bad Calibration

  • All gases drifting together
  • Unrealistic correlations between components
  • Consistent bias across operating range
  • Audit failures despite stable readings

Audit & Compliance Best Practices

  • Maintain calibration and validation records
  • Keep certificates for all calibration gases
  • Demonstrate validation trend stability
  • Explain moisture handling strategy clearly

Auditors trust stable validation history more than frequent recalibration.

Gas Chromatograph (GC) – Basics

Category: Analyzer · Gas Chromatograph · Fundamentals

What a Gas Chromatograph Does

A Gas Chromatograph (GC) is an analytical instrument used to separate, identify, and quantify individual components in a gas or vapor sample.

In process plants, refineries, and petrochemical units, GCs are mainly used for:

  • Online composition measurement
  • Product quality monitoring
  • Process control and optimization
  • Safety and regulatory compliance

Working Principle (Step-by-Step)

The GC works on the principle of separation by retention time. Each component in the sample travels through the column at a different speed, depending on its interaction with the column material.

  1. Sample is injected into the GC through a sampling system or valve
  2. Carrier gas pushes the sample into the column
  3. Components separate inside the column based on boiling point and polarity
  4. Separated components reach the detector at different times
  5. Detector converts concentration into an electrical signal
  6. Signal is processed and displayed as peaks on a chromatogram

Main GC Components (Field View)

1. Carrier Gas System

The carrier gas is the mobile phase that transports the sample through the column. Common carrier gases include:

  • Helium – stable and widely used
  • Hydrogen – fast analysis but higher safety risk
  • Nitrogen – economical but slower separation

Poor carrier gas purity is one of the most common causes of baseline noise and detector contamination.

2. Sample Injection System

Online GCs use sampling valves, loops, and regulators to introduce a fixed volume of sample.

  • Sample pressure stability is critical
  • Leaks cause retention time shift
  • Condensation leads to negative or distorted peaks

3. Column & Oven

The column is the heart of the GC. It is installed inside a temperature-controlled oven.

  • Column type determines separation quality
  • Oven temperature affects retention time
  • Temperature overshoot can damage columns

4. Detector

The detector senses separated components and converts them into electrical signals.

  • FID – hydrocarbons
  • TCD – permanent gases
  • ECD – trace compounds

Calibration & Validation

Calibration ensures accuracy and repeatability of GC measurements. It is typically done using certified calibration gas mixtures.

  • Zero calibration checks baseline stability
  • Span calibration verifies concentration accuracy
  • Response factors must be periodically validated
  • Calibration failures often indicate sampling issues

Common GC Problems Seen in Plants

  • Baseline drift due to carrier gas contamination
  • Retention time shift from pressure or temperature changes
  • Poor peak separation caused by column aging
  • Negative peaks due to condensation or valve leakage
  • Detector noise from unstable power or grounding

Basic Troubleshooting Approach

When a GC shows abnormal behavior, troubleshooting should follow a logical sequence:

  1. Confirm carrier gas pressure and purity
  2. Check for leaks in sampling lines and valves
  3. Verify oven and detector temperatures
  4. Review recent calibration history
  5. Inspect chromatogram trends, not just alarms

Most GC problems in the field originate from the sampling system, not the analyzer itself.

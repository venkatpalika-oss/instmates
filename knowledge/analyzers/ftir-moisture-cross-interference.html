FTIR Moisture & Cross-Interference Explained

Category: Analyzer · FTIR · Measurement Accuracy

Why Moisture Is the Biggest Enemy of FTIR

In FTIR analyzers, water vapor (H₂O) is not just another gas. It has very strong and broad infrared absorption bands that overlap with many target gases.

Because FTIR measures absorption across a wide IR spectrum, moisture can easily interfere with:

  • SO₂, NO, NO₂
  • CO and CO₂
  • NH₃ and HCl
  • Hydrocarbon components

How FTIR “Sees” Gas Molecules

Each gas absorbs infrared energy at specific wavelengths, creating a unique absorption fingerprint. FTIR software identifies gases by matching these fingerprints.

Moisture becomes a problem because:

  • Its absorption bands are wide
  • It absorbs strongly even at low concentrations
  • It overlaps with many other gases

What Is Cross-Interference?

Cross-interference occurs when absorption from one gas affects the measurement of another gas.

  • Moisture masks weaker gas absorption
  • One gas appears higher when another increases
  • False alarms during humidity changes
  • Calibration drift without actual process change

Common Field Symptoms of Moisture Interference

  • SO₂ or NOx readings increase during rainy or humid conditions
  • Analyzer values change only at night
  • Readings jump after startup or shutdown
  • Calibration fails even with correct gases
  • Different gases trend together unrealistically

Why Problems Appear During Night or Winter

Many technicians observe FTIR issues only during night shifts. This is usually due to:

  • Ambient temperature drop
  • Condensation in sample lines
  • Heater power cycling
  • Increased relative humidity

How Moisture Interference Is Managed

1. Proper Sampling Design

  • Heated probes and lines above dew point
  • No cold spots or low points
  • Short sample paths

2. Moisture Compensation Models

  • FTIR software mathematically compensates H₂O
  • Requires stable moisture behavior
  • Fails when moisture changes rapidly

3. Calibration Strategy

  • Calibration gases should match moisture conditions
  • Dry calibration on wet process causes errors
  • Post-maintenance validation is mandatory

Troubleshooting Moisture & Cross-Interference

  1. Trend moisture and target gases together
  2. Check sample line temperature at multiple points
  3. Inspect filters for moisture saturation
  4. Review raw FTIR spectrum if available
  5. Compare readings before and after drying

If multiple gases change together without a process reason, moisture interference should be suspected immediately.

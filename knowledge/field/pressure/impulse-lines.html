Impulse Lines – Wet Leg, Dry Leg & Field Problems

Impulse lines connect the process to the pressure transmitter. Most pressure measurement errors originate here — not inside the transmitter.

Types of Impulse Line Installations

Wet Leg

Used mainly in closed vessels. Both impulse lines remain filled with liquid to create a stable reference.

  • Common in steam & condensate service
  • Requires constant fill level
  • Sensitive to leaks and evaporation

Dry Leg

Used in open tanks. One side is vented to atmosphere.

  • Simpler installation
  • Condensation is main enemy
  • Incorrect slope causes false readings

Slope & Routing Rules (Critical)

  • Impulse lines must slope away from the transmitter
  • Avoid pockets, loops, and dead legs
  • Both legs should be equal length where possible
  • Provide drain and vent points

Common Impulse Line Problems

Symptom Likely Cause Field Check
Slow response Plugging or wax buildup Crack impulse line carefully
Zero shift Condensate imbalance Drain both legs
No reading Frozen or blocked line Check heat tracing

Field Tips from Experience

  • Open impulse valves slowly
  • Flush lines before calibration
  • Mark High (H) and Low (L) lines clearly
  • Insulate both legs equally
